Simple Tooth Removal

Your dentist will offer you an injection of anesthesia to numb the area from where the tooth is going to be extracted. Sometimes the dentist might also use strong general anesthetic; it will make the procedure painless as you will get sleep.

Do I Have The Option To Replace My Missing Tooth?

When you discuss the treatment plan with the dentist, they will explain the available options.

Make sure that your dentist knows about your medical history. Also, ask the dentist to tell you a pre-treatment estimate; so you can be prepared with that.

Causes Of Tooth Extraction

Here are a few conditions when you should consider tooth extraction in order to reduce the pain.

  • Taking off a fractured or distorted tooth
  • Getting a patient ready for orthodontic treatment
  • A progressive periodontal condition that has loosened the tooth roots
  • Baby teeth or extra teeth that hinder adult teeth
  • Serious tooth decay cannot be cured with root canal therapy.

Surgical Tooth Removal

People often opt for wisdom tooth removal, that is the third set of molars. Most people have a minimum of one impacted molar, and there is not enough space to grow naturally. In that case, the best option is surgical tooth removal.

Once you get your wisdom teeth removed, you can ensure they don’t harm the surrounding teeth and bones. You will notice bleeding after the tooth extraction, which is normal. In addition, there will be pinkish-tinted saliva and subtle oozing from your mouth for the first 36 hours.

The procedure is complex, so the dentist will sedate you before performing the surgery. They use a dental drill, use pressure on your tooth with an extraction forceps or elevator, and remove your tooth. You won't feel any pain but you can expect to feel pressure against the tooth. Some patients have found this experience unpleasant but it will help you alleviate the pain.

Bone Grafting for Dental Implants

A dental implant is an excellent, safe, and long-term alternative for someone in good general health who loses a tooth due to trauma, periodontitis or another infection.
A Board Certified Specialist in "Smiles by Gentle Dental" places an artificial root called an implant in the jaw to support a replacement tooth or bridge there. Once the procedure is complete, implants behave precisely like natural teeth regarding feel, appearance, and function. When an implant is being inserted, bone grafting may also be required as part of the procedure.

When is bone Grafting Necessary?

If a patient doesn't have enough strong, healthy natural bones in their mouth to support the dental implants, bone grafting may be an option. These factors are frequently to blame for this lack of natural bones:

  • Any developmental disturbances 
  • Dental Illness
  • Injury or trauma to the face
  • Vacant space after extraction 

What Causes Bone Loss When Teeth Fall Out?

Your teeth's support and retention are the responsibilities of the alveolar bone. Alveolar bone begins to atrophy if teeth are absent for a long time.Remember that for dental implants to function, there must be osseointegration—that is, the implant and bone must unite to form a solid base for the crown. 

How Is Tori Surgery Performed?

High-speed dental drill bits and CA chisels are frequently used in tori surgery. Your "Smiles by Gentle Dental" professional uses local anesthetics and, if necessary, sedation to ensure you don't feel anything during the treatment.

Do You Need Tori Removal?

The majority of tori patients have mild cases and won't need treatment. However, palatinus excision or mandibular torus surgery may also be necessary to:

  • Treating discomfort or agony
  • Put on the lower or upper dentures.
  • Fit flippers or top or lower partial dentures.
  • Prevent periodontal pockets from growing.

What Is Dental Trauma?

Dental trauma refers to physical damage to the teeth, alveolar bone, gums, or soft oral tissues, such as the tongue and lips. Dental trauma can result from several different types of injuries, including the ones listed below:

  • The tooth was inserted improperly (intrusion)
  • Broken tooth socket
  • Knocked-out tooth (subluxation)
  • knocked out tooth (avulsion)
  • Injuries to the gums
  • Tooth fracture
  • Injuries to the gums

Types of Dental Trauma?

Superficial fracture only affects the tooth's enamel, the tough outer layer. Unless the fracture leaves the tooth with a pointed edge, this is typically insignificant, even though it is frequently simple to file down a sharp edge.

A more significant fracture may also be present if your tooth feels sensitive to touch, heat, or cold. Because of this, the pulp and dentine, two of the tooth's more delicate inside regions, were exposed. As a result, bacterial infections will become more likely.

Displacement:

A more severe knock may dislodge the tooth, causing it to dangle loosely from the socket or sink further into it. Another possibility for the tooth is side displacement. The delicate blood veins supplying the pulp are typically injured in cases of tooth displacement, necessitating a specialized treatment for the tooth.
